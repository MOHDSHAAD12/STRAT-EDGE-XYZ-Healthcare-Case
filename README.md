2. Our Diagnosis — What Was Actually Wrong
The first instinct in a case like this is to say "AI failed." We argued the opposite.

XYZ's AI is technically working. The problem is structural — the organisation was never designed to actually use it.

The improvements are real: costs down, wait times down, diagnostic accuracy up. The gap is not in the technology. It is in how the technology was deployed, governed, and adopted.
The $25M Gap — Broken Down
$25 million in value is sitting unrealised inside XYZ's existing AI infrastructure. It is not gone — it is inaccessible. The question is not "should we invest in more AI?" It is "why can't we access the value we already paid for?"
We identified four structural root causes.

Root Cause 1 — AI Lives Outside the EHR
Every clinical AI tool at XYZ was deployed as a standalone platform. To act on an AI recommendation, a clinician had to leave their primary system, open a separate tool, review the output, return to the EHR, and manually enter any decision.
This creates friction that clinicians will rationally avoid. Research shows hospitals with AI bundled into Epic EHR achieve 90% adoption. Standalone tools average 50%. XYZ is standalone across the board.
This is the single biggest structural failure in the entire case.

Root Cause 2 — Clinicians Were Never in the Room
Clinical AI — tools that directly affect diagnostic decisions — was procured and deployed by IT and administrative teams. Senior doctors had almost no involvement in design or evaluation.
The result: 38% of staff trust AI recommendations. This is not a cultural problem or a resistance problem. It is a governance failure. You cannot put tools into a clinical environment without clinical co-design and expect trust to follow.
McKinsey (2025) confirms that high-performing organisations are 3× more likely to have strong domain expert ownership of AI — not just IT ownership.

Root Cause 3 — One Training Session in Two Years
Formal training was a single two-day onboarding at rollout in 2023. Nothing since — no refreshers, no role-specific modules, no continuous learning. By 2025, 59% of staff feel undertrained.
This is not a training gap. It is a policy failure. Deploying a complex, evolving AI system and providing one orientation session is orientation, not training.
CommonSpirit Health launched an AI Academy with 9 courses and 16 micro-learning modules. Year one: 5,000 registrations, 1,400 employees upskilled, measurable adoption improvement across departments.

Root Cause 4 — No Prioritisation
The $50M was distributed across all three AI domains simultaneously. No framework determined which use cases to fund first or defer.
This matters because payback timelines differ significantly:

Admin AI (billing, RCM): 12–18 months
Operational AI (bed management): 12–18 months
Clinical AI (diagnostic support): 24–36 months

By treating all three as equal priorities, XYZ front-loaded spend into longer-payback categories without securing fast wins first.

4. Our Framework — The AI Value Leakage Model
Rather than applying a standard MBA framework (SWOT, Porter's, McKinsey 7S), we built one specific to this problem.
The AI Value Leakage Model — the premise being that XYZ is not failing to generate AI value. It is leaking it through three identifiable gaps. Plug the leaks, and the value already paid for becomes accessible.

                  AI VALUE LEAKAGE MODEL                      

💡 Our Framework — The AI Value Leakage Model
We built a custom framework for this problem. The core idea: XYZ is not failing to generate AI value — it is leaking it through three identifiable gaps.

Leakage 01 — Integration Failure
Problem: AI tools ran outside doctors' primary systems, forcing platform switching that clinicians rationally avoided.
Fix: Deep EHR integration — make AI invisible inside the existing workflow.

Leakage 02 — Adoption Collapse
Problem: Only 38% of staff trust AI. High override rates mean the $50M investment sits idle.
Fix: AI Academy + clinical co-design — rebuild trust through training and involvement.

Leakage 03 — Wrong Prioritisation
Problem: Budget spread across all AI domains at once. Slow-payback areas funded before fast ones.
Fix: Focus entirely on the top 2 fast-ROI use cases first.

The result: $25M captured. $25M still leaking — waiting to be recovered.
Our recommendation: Stop buying new AI. Start recovering the value from the AI you already own.
Why a custom framework:
Standard frameworks are taught to every MBA student. Judges see them in every presentation. A framework built for the specific problem — using the specific numbers from this case — demonstrates analytical thinking rather than template application. The "leakage" framing also shifts the conversation from "AI failed" to "value can be recovered" — which made our solution feel achievable, not aspirational.

7. Implementation Roadmap — 3 Phases, 18 Months
Phase 1 — Stabilise (0–3 Months)
Remove the structural barriers actively suppressing ROI.

Clinical Council: Senior doctors lead AI redesign and integration decisions — no more IT-only procurement
Shadow Clinician Programme: IT developers observe real-world clinical workflows before designing integrations (NCBI best practice, 2025)
EHR Integration Sprint: Top two AI tools (billing + bed management) integrated into existing EHR — platform switching eliminated
Internal Rebrand: AI positioned as 'Clinical Co-Pilot' — reduces workload, does not replace judgement. Directly addresses the 57% job security fear.

Target KPI: Override rate down 20% within 90 days

Phase 2 — Scale Value (3–9 Months)
Capture ROI from the two highest-priority use cases.

AI Academy Launch: 9 courses, 16 micro-learning modules (CommonSpirit model). Target: 1,000+ employees enrolled. Role-specific tracks for doctors, nurses, and admin.
Full RCM + Billing Integration: Target $5–8M in Year 1 savings from improved charge capture and reduced coding errors
Bed Management Scale-Up: Push utilisation 74% → 82%. Estimated revenue impact: ~$15M
Incentive Alignment: Department bonuses tied to AI adoption KPIs — override rates, data quality scores

Target KPI: ROI lifts from 0.5× to 1.5× by Month 9

Phase 3 — Optimise (9–18 Months)
Extend the model to clinical AI and build long-term data advantage.

Clinical AI — Full EHR Embed: Radiology, pathology, sepsis detection fully integrated. Trust rebuilt through Phase 1–2 makes this viable now.
AI Centre of Excellence (CoE): Governance moves from IT to a cross-functional CoE — responsible for evaluating, procuring, and scaling all future AI
ABDM Integration: Link XYZ's 1.8M patient dataset to India's 799M national health IDs. Creates a predictive data moat smaller competitors cannot replicate.
KPI Shift: Reporting moves from wait times → EBITDA margin per procedure

Target KPI: 3.2× ROI benchmark by Month 18

8. Change Management — The Trust Equation
Change management is the most mentioned topic in AI case competitions — and the most superficially answered. We treated it as a hard financial metric, not a soft HR initiative.
The Paradox in XYZ's Own Data

62% say AI helps their productivity
38% trust AI recommendations
57% fear AI costs them their job

Read together: most of XYZ's workforce is already benefiting from AI but doesn't trust it and fears it. This is not contradiction — it is the result of zero communication strategy and zero reskilling commitment. The evidence already supports the message. The internal campaign just hasn't happened.
Four Non-Negotiable Conditions for Clinician Trust
1. Explainability
Clinicians need the why behind a recommendation, not just the output. Black-box AI — "this patient is high-risk" with no explanation — gets overridden. Hospitals investing in Explainable AI (XAI) consistently show higher adoption.
2. EHR Integration
Already a structural fix — but also a trust signal. A tool that requires extra steps communicates that it was not designed for clinical reality. Integration signals institutional commitment.
3. Clinical Co-Design
Documented as the single most consistent predictor of adoption across NCBI (2025), McKinsey (2025), and AMA guidance. No workarounds.
4. Continuous Training
Not an event. A system. CommonSpirit's AI Academy generated a 5× increase in care gap closure orders in year one. One-time onboarding does not change behaviour. Structured continuous learning does.
The Retention Argument
An argument rarely included in competing analyses — but financially significant:
Replacing one physician costs 2–3× their annual salary (Healthcare Dive, 2025). AI clinical documentation tools reduce after-hours 'pajama time' by 48–60% (University of Vermont; Corewell Health). This directly reduces burnout — the leading driver of physician attrition.
We are not just making doctors happier. We are protecting XYZ's most expensive asset — and the $50M investment depends on these clinicians being willing to use the tools.
The IBM Watson Warning
IBM Watson Health invested $5B in healthcare AI and largely dismantled it by 2022. The post-mortem is consistent: no EHR integration, black-box recommendations clinicians didn't trust, and no clear ROI narrative for hospital leadership.
XYZ is showing all three warning signs. The difference: there is still time to correct course.
Our line to the panel: "XYZ is not IBM Watson — yet. But it is showing every early warning sign that preceded Watson's failure. Our plan is what Watson's never had: a structured adoption and integration strategy built before the damage becomes irreversible."

9. Reflection — What Worked and What We Would Do Differently
What Worked
Diagnostic-first approach. We resisted jumping to solutions for the first 30–40 minutes. Spending real time on why the problem exists led us to a diagnosis that was specific, defensible, and different from generic frameworks.
Financial specificity. Every projection was benchmarked against a named source. When the panel pushed on numbers, we could name the origin. That changed the quality of the conversation.
The IBM Watson reference. This was the moment in the presentation where the room shifted. It demonstrated knowledge of the problem's history — not just the case in front of us.
One bold call. Recommending a pause on new AI investments was counterintuitive and direct. The panel engaged with it rather than nodding past it.
One hero number per slide. Each slide was anchored to a single number — $25M gap, 38% trust, 0.5× ROI. Scannable under time pressure. Memorable after the presentation ends.
Custom framework. The AI Value Leakage Model was built for this problem. It showed the panel we had moved from applying frameworks to building them.
What We Would Do Differently
We over-researched market size. Early preparation included global market figures ($500B AI healthcare market, 44% CAGR) that added no value to the core argument. We cut most of it, but lost time that could have gone into sharper financial modelling.
The change management ROI could have been quantified more precisely. We made the physician retention argument verbally but did not put a specific dollar figure on it for XYZ's scale. With 22 hospitals and an estimable physician headcount, this was calculable — and would have sharpened Slide 4.
We could have been more explicit about assumptions upfront. Stating assumptions clearly before presenting numbers makes projections more defensible under questioning — not less. We did this partially, but could have led with it more confidently.
