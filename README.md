“XYZ Healthcare doesn’t have an AI problem.

What this repository is about
This repository documents our full journey during the STRAT_EDGE case competition—from receiving the case brief to presenting our solution in front of the judges.
We’ve tried to go beyond just sharing slides. Instead, this is a complete breakdown of how we thought through the problem.


It includes:
How we approached the case initially
The diagnostic and root cause analysis
The framework we built (instead of using standard ones)
Financial reasoning and ROI estimates
Implementation roadmap
And finally, what worked and what we would do differently

The original case brief and final PPT and market research pdf are also attached.


The case in simple terms
XYZ Healthcare invested $50M in AI over two years.
On paper, things improved:
Wait times reduced
Costs came down
Diagnostic accuracy improved
Revenue increased slightly

But financially:
Total value created = $25M
Total investment = $50M
ROI = 0.5×

So the main question was:
Why is AI working operationally, but not financially?

Our starting point
We deliberately did not jump into solutions.
For the first 30–40 minutes, we only focused on understanding the problem.

At first, it looked like:
“AI is not working properly”
But after going deeper, we realised:
The AI is actually working.
The problem is that it is not being used properly—and more importantly, it was never designed to be used in real workflows.

What we think is actually wrong

We identified a few key issues.
1. AI is not integrated into the main system (EHR)
Doctors have to:
leave their main system
open another platform
check AI output
come back and apply it
In practice, this just doesn’t work.
What stood out to us here was that this is not about laziness—it’s about workflow friction.
If something adds extra steps, it will simply not be used.

2. Doctors were not involved in the process
Most AI tools were selected and deployed by IT or admin teams.
So naturally:
only 38% of staff trust AI
This doesn’t feel like a “culture problem” to us.
It looks more like a design and governance issue.

3. Training was almost non-existent
There was:
one 2-day session in 2023
nothing after that
By 2025:
~59% of staff still feel undertrained
This felt less like a training gap and more like a missing system.

4. Everything was deployed at once
There was no prioritisation.
Different AI systems have very different payback periods:
Admin AI → fast returns
Operational AI → moderate
Clinical AI → slower
But XYZ invested in everything at the same time.
Looking back, this seems like one of the key reasons why ROI stayed low.


Our core idea: AI Value Leakage
Instead of saying “AI is failing”, we reframed the problem.

Value is being created—but it is leaking before being captured.
We identified three main leakages:
Integration failure
Adoption gap
Wrong prioritisation

Fixing these doesn’t require new AI.
It requires fixing how existing AI is used.


Our main recommendation
One of the more direct calls we made was:
Pause new AI investments for 6 months.
Focus only on making existing systems work.
This was slightly risky to say, but we felt it was necessary.
Buying more AI while operating at low ROI felt like:
adding more water to a leaking bucket


Financial thinking 
We did not try to create aggressive projections.
Instead, we built our estimates using benchmark data from real hospital systems and industry studies, and then adjusted them to XYZ’s scale.

Our estimate:
Administrative AI (RCM, billing, coding) → $12–18M annually
Based on:
~15% improvement in charge capture and reduction in coding errors
Evidence from Axis Intelligence (2025) and Strativera (2025) on hospital revenue cycle optimisation

Bed management & readmission reduction → $8–12M annually
Based on:
Improving utilisation from 74% toward ~80–85%
Studies from Johns Hopkins / PMC (2025) showing revenue impact from improved bed turnover

Internal scaling based on XYZ’s ~$400M revenue base
Clinical AI (once integrated into workflow) → $5–8M annually
Based on:
Efficiency improvements and reduction in length of stay
Findings from arXiv (2025) and NIH-backed studies on diagnostic support systems

So overall:
$25M–$38M potential annual value unlock

Payback expectation:
18–24 months, assuming proper execution

Important clarification
We were careful not to present these numbers as guaranteed outcomes.

These are:
Benchmark-aligned estimates, not best-case scenarios
Dependent on integration quality, adoption levels, and execution discipline
One thing we tried to be careful about

We avoided using:
aggressive growth assumptions
unrealistic adoption rates
Most of our numbers assume:
partial improvement—not perfect execution


Implementation approach

We broke this into 3 phases:

Phase 1 (0–3 months)
Fix integration
Involve doctors
Reduce friction

Phase 2 (3–9 months)
Focus on high ROI areas
Launch structured training (AI Academy)
Align incentives

Phase 3 (9–18 months)
Expand clinical AI
Build long-term AI capability
Improve data integration

One important insight (change management)
Something that became very clear to us:
People were already using AI…
but they didn’t trust it.
62% said it improves productivity
Only 38% trust it
57% fear job loss

This combination is interesting.
It suggests the problem is not resistance—it’s communication and design.


Why we used IBM Watson

We included the IBM Watson example very intentionally.
Not as a story—but as a warning.
Watson failed mainly because:
it wasn’t integrated into workflows
doctors didn’t trust it
ROI was unclear

And we noticed:
XYZ is showing similar early signals
That comparison helped us strengthen our argument.


What worked for us
Spending time on diagnosis before jumping to solutions
Using numbers we could defend
Making one bold recommendation (pause AI spending)
Keeping slides simple (one key number per slide)

What we would do differently
We initially spent too much time on general market research
We could have quantified change management impact more clearly
One Q&A question on “how to measure override rates” caught us slightly off guard


Final thought

This case changed how we think about AI in business.
It’s not about building better technology.
It’s about making sure the technology actually gets used.
